# 2. Validated Learning over Assumptions

We prioritize evidence from real usage over untested beliefs.
Unvalidated ideas are hypotheses, not strategy.

- _Decision check:_ Name the specific signal (data or observed user behavior — first-hand or from a named comparable) that supports this. **Stated preferences ("users said they want it," "the CEO is excited") do not count as a signal.** If you can't name behavioral evidence, it is an assumption.
- _Status:_ enforceable.

## Notes

- The distinction that matters is **behavior vs. stated preference**. Two
  years of reflection (see Origin) show that excitement, agreement in
  meetings, and "yes, we'd use that" responses repeatedly failed to
  predict adoption. Behavior — repeat usage, prior pilot retention, an
  observable workaround the user has built without us — is what counts.
- Second-hand behavioral evidence (a comparable org's published usage
  data, an analogous product's documented retention) is acceptable when
  named explicitly. Second-hand stated preferences are not.
- The boundary with [#9 Aligned Incentives over Stated Needs](./09-aligned-incentives-over-stated-needs.md):
  #2 says evidence must exist and must be behavioral. #9 says the
  evidence must describe an active incentive that will keep the user
  doing the behavior. They are complementary checks.

## Open questions

- How do we handle decisions that genuinely have no prior signal — e.g., a
  net-new product idea? Is the answer always "run a small experiment first"
  (i.e., principle #3), or are there exceptions?
